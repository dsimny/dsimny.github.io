How the engine works

Think of the engine as a flight simulator for tonight's games. Instead of predicting one outcome, it plays each game 10,000 times and counts what happens. A team that wins 6,110 of 10,000 simulations is a 61.1% team, and the fair line falls straight out of the count. No gut feelings, no vibes; arithmetic.

What each simulation knows

  • Team run rates: runs scored and allowed per game for both clubs, live from the MLB Stats API, normalized to the league average.
  • Starting pitchers: each starter's ERA vs league, weighted over the ~5.5 innings starters actually cover; the bullpen inherits the team's overall run prevention.
  • Park factors: Coors Field is a hot-air balloon (1.24); T-Mobile Park is a walk-in freezer (0.92). Static season approximations, refreshed manually.
  • Home-field advantage: an evidence-sized bump (~54% for an even matchup), not the folk-wisdom 60%.
  • Fat-tailed scoring: runs come in bunches, so scores are drawn from a negative binomial distribution that allows crooked innings and blowouts, not a tidy bell curve.

The market is on the card

A probability alone isn't a bet; a bet is a probability versus a price. Each game carries the market line and three numbers computed from it: Edge (our win probability minus the probability implied by the offered price), EV per unit, and a quarter-Kelly stake suggestion, always capped by the risk-tier framework. The governor beats the gas pedal. Two hard gates follow: the edge gate (no allocation under a 2-point edge; a good side at a bad price is a bad bet) and Rule 8, the Divergence Governor: when our model and the de-vigged market disagree by more than 12 points, we assume the market knows something our inputs don't (lineups, injury news, form), and the play is held for manual review instead of bet harder. A model that never doubts itself is a tout with extra steps.

Then the circuit breakers get a veto

The model proposes; eight risk rules dispose. Games with TBD starters are scratched, limited-workload starters trigger downgrades, capped juice forces run-line pivots, and every check, passed or failed, is printed on every card. That's the product.

What this engine does not do: read this before betting

  • Market lines are a snapshot, not a live tick-by-tick feed. Lines move, and the price at your book may differ. Confirm the number before you bet it.
  • Telemetry rules are manual. Statcast velocity/spin trends and rolling road wOBA (Rules 3, 5, 6) aren't automated yet; they're flagged for human review, never silently claimed.
  • Not modeled: lineups, rest, umpires, weather. Park factors are static season approximations.
Why publish our limitations next to our picks? Because a pick site that hides its wiring is asking you to bet on a magic trick. Ours is an aquarium; the whole tank is behind glass, down to the per-date random seed (20260722) that makes every day's board reproducible.

The eight circuit breakers

Bankroll rules work like the breaker panel in your house: any single circuit can fail without burning the place down. These run on every pick, every day.

Rule 2: High-Juice Favorite Cap Automated · market lines

No straight moneylines on road favorites at −180 or heavier, or home favorites past −220, evaluated against the actual market price. The play pivots to the −1.5 run line or passes. Laying heavy juice is renting a favorite at luxury prices: the wins are small and the losses are structural.

Rule 4: Injury Return Protocol Automated (heuristic)

Mandatory volume freeze on pitchers fresh off the IL or on restricted workloads. Heuristic: a starter far under the expected innings load this deep in the season triggers the freeze and a one-tier confidence downgrade.

Rule 5: Trailing Telemetry Deviation Penalty Manual review

Sharp velocity, spin, or efficiency drops over a starter's trailing three outings trigger a downgrade or fade. Requires a Statcast feed, so it is flagged for human review for now.

Rule 6: Road wOBA Suppression Multiplier Manual review

A 12% projected-scoring tax on visitors whose rolling 3-game road wOBA trails league baseline by .035+. Requires rolling splits, so it is manual for now.

Rule 7: Late-Line Circuit Breaker Automated

Any game with a TBD starter inside the pre-game window is scratched automatically. No named starter, no position: you don't board a flight with an unnamed pilot.

Rule 8: Divergence Governor Automated

If the model's win probability and the de-vigged market disagree by more than 12 points, the play is held for manual review, with no allocation, no matter how juicy the "edge" looks. A huge gap isn't a gift; it's a warning that the market has priced in something our inputs haven't seen. The best defense against a model's blind spots is respecting the one opponent that never sleeps.

Rookie Ambush Overhaul & Two-Out NRFI Override Dormant

NRFI/early-under governors (rookie launch-angle variance; #2–3 slot ISO vs high-ride fastballs). Open Ledger doesn't publish NRFI positions yet, so these breakers are dormant until that market ships.

Edge Gate & Bankroll Governor Automated

No allocation under a 2-point edge versus the offered price. Sizing is the lesser of the confidence tier (3u / 2u / 1u) and a quarter-Kelly stake, with one-tier downgrades on flags and a hard daily ceiling of 10% of bankroll across all published plays.
